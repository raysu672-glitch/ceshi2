Reading Passage 1

The Commercial Space Industry

Since the Space Age began in the 1950s, space exploration has largely been the domain of government agencies like NASA and Roscosmos. However, in recent years, a new player has entered the field: the commercial space industry. Private companies such as SpaceX, Blue Origin, and Virgin Galactic are now developing their own rockets, spacecraft, and even plans for space tourism.

The business model might not be perfect, but the commercial space sector has attracted billions of dollars in investment. Companies are betting that there is a significant market for launching satellites, carrying cargo to the International Space Station, and eventually taking paying passengers on suborbital flights.

However, the industry is not without risks. If there was another accident, it could set back the entire commercial space programme by years. The memory of the Challenger and Columbia disasters still looms large, and any similar incident involving a private company would raise serious questions about safety standards.

Meanwhile, NASA has its own challenges. Experts doubt whether NASA can fulfil its ambitions to return humans to the Moon and eventually send astronauts to Mars, given its current budget constraints and shifting political priorities. NASA has often been under political pressure, with each new administration bringing different goals and funding levels.

Some observers worry that the development of the commercial space industry may lead to a space race between NASA and the private sector, with both competing for limited resources and talent. Others argue that cooperation between the two could accelerate progress and make space more accessible than ever before.

Reading Passage 2

How to Spot a Liar

Deception is a widespread phenomenon in the animal kingdom. All living animals can lie, from the smallest insects to the largest mammals. Some animals use camouflage to hide from predators, while others produce false signals to attract mates or confuse rivals.

Human beings are no exception. People tell lies for a variety of reasons. Some people tell lies for self-preservation, to avoid punishment or protect themselves from harm. Others lie to gain social advantage, to impress others, or to maintain relationships.

Researchers have long been interested in understanding the mechanisms behind deception. Some studies suggest that lying activates different parts of the brain than truth-telling, though the exact neural pathways remain a subject of ongoing investigation.

The ability to detect lies is considered by many researchers to be just as important as the ability to tell them. However, studies have shown that most people are actually quite poor at detecting deception, with accuracy rates barely above chance level.

To be a good liar, one has to understand other people's emotions and thought processes. This requires a degree of empathy and social intelligence that not everyone possesses. The most convincing liars are those who can anticipate what their audience wants to hear and tailor their stories accordingly.

Reading Passage 3

Bird Migration

Paragraph A: Birds are remarkable creatures that have evolved an extraordinary range of physical adaptations to enable them to undertake long-distance migrations. Many migratory birds have lightweight, streamlined bodies and powerful wings that allow them to cover thousands of kilometres. Some species have specially adapted respiratory systems that enable them to fly at high altitudes where oxygen levels are low. Their feathers are designed to provide both insulation and aerodynamic efficiency, making long flights possible.

Paragraph B: The primary reason why birds migrate is to find food and suitable breeding grounds. As seasons change, food sources in one region become scarce, prompting birds to travel to areas where resources are more abundant. In spring, many species fly north to take advantage of the insect-rich environments of the northern hemisphere for raising their young. In autumn, they return to warmer southern regions where food remains available during the winter months.

Paragraph C: Interestingly, some migratory birds fly much further than necessary to reach their destination. Researchers have observed that certain species bypass closer feeding grounds in favour of more distant locations, even when the nearer sites appear to offer adequate resources. The reasons for this behaviour remain unclear, though scientists speculate that it may be related to genetic programming or the avoidance of competition at popular stopover sites.

Paragraph D: One of the most fascinating aspects of bird migration is how young birds find their way without any prior experience or guidance. Species such as cuckoos, which are raised by foster parents of a different species, migrate alone to their wintering grounds without parental guidance. They rely on an innate sense of direction that scientists believe is encoded in their genes, allowing them to navigate across continents to destinations they have never seen before.

Paragraph E: Recent research has shed new light on the mechanisms of bird navigation. Scientists have discovered that birds can tell directions like a compass by observing the sun and the stars. Some species also appear to use the Earth's magnetic field as a navigational aid, with specialised cells in their eyes or beaks that act as biological compasses. Birds flying at night are thought to use celestial navigation, and studies suggest they may need less water during nocturnal flights due to cooler temperatures.

Paragraph F: Weather conditions play a crucial role in determining the success of bird migration. Strong headwinds can force birds to make unscheduled stops, while favourable tailwinds can significantly speed up their journey. Some species are remarkably resistant to strong winds and can continue flying in conditions that would ground most other birds. However, extreme weather events such as storms can disrupt migration patterns, causing birds to deviate from their usual routes and sometimes leading to significant mortality.
